§ 01

What C actually is

C is not a curiosity. It is the long-run fraction of residues on a modular ring that survive a lift — the exact proportion of integers coprime to M that are also coprime to M+1, averaged over all M as M grows large. Every prime in existence has already shaped this number. Its decimal expansion is a compressed record of the entire prime distribution.

The identity C = ζ(2) · dFT is not an approximation. It is exact. The Riemann zeta value ζ(2) = π²/6 encodes the aggregate weight of all primes squared. The Feller–Tornier constant dFT measures the density of consecutive squarefree integers. A single p² cancels term by term, and what remains is C.

The identity — one line from definitions ζ(2) · dFT = ∏p [p²/(p²−1)] · [(p²−2)/p²]
              = ∏p (p²−2)/(p²−1)
              = C ← the p² cancels exactly

Because ζ(2) = π²/6 is computable to millions of decimal places, C can be known to hundreds of thousands of decimal places without sieving for a single new prime.

§ 02

The function C(x) — a smooth field

C is the limit. C(x) is the function that approaches it — a smooth, continuous Euler product defined for every real x ≥ 0. Between exits, C(x) is perfectly smooth. It is a field under tension, and the primes are the only things that disturb it.

§ 03

D(x) — the sonar equation

Take the log-derivative of C(x) and negate it. What emerges is D(x) — smooth everywhere except at one class of points. At x = p−1, for each prime p, the active term exits. Its value at exit is exactly 1/p(p−1). D(x) jumps.

D(x) — the log-derivative D(x) = −d/dx log C(x) = Σp > x+1 1/(p²−x−1)

Jump at x = p−1:  ΔD = 1/p(p−1) = s
At x = m−1, m composite:  ΔD = 0 exactly
The sonar principle

C(x) is built from modular ring geometry — not from a list of primes. The discontinuities of D(x) turn out to be at exactly the primes — nowhere else. The primes are not put in. They come out. C(x) is the medium. The primes are the disturbance. D(x) is the signal.

The jump size s = 1/p(p−1) is the signature of prime p. Measure the jump, recover the prime: p = (1 + √(1 + 4/s)) / 2.

§ 04

Why C is a cheat code

Normally, knowing something about the billionth prime requires traversing every prime before it. C breaks this. Because C encodes the collective impact of all primes simultaneously, its decimal expansion carries information about primes that have never been explicitly computed.

The decoding reframe

Finding primes is normally a search problem. The framework reframes it as a decoding problem: a high-resolution photograph of the haystack exists, and the needles are specific pixels following a geometric rule. The photograph is C. The geometric rule is s = 1/p(p−1).

§ 05

What C is — and what it is not

C does not predict primes. D(x) has its discontinuities at the primes, but recovering which specific integers are prime still requires evaluating D(x) at specific points — which requires knowing the primes. The inversion formula p(s) is a verifier, not a generator.

The source code metaphor

C is not the primes. C is the compiled output of the rule that generates the primes. The source code is modular ring geometry. C is what that source code evaluates to when run over all primes simultaneously. It is a fixed point of the system.

§ 06

The Golden Ratio Sentinel

At s = 1, the inversion formula gives φ = (1+√5)/2 = 1.6180339887… — the positive root of the Fibonacci characteristic equation x² − x − 1 = 0. No prime produces s = 1. The golden ratio is the permanent unreachable boundary.

The Fibonacci sequence and the prime number sequence are both governed by the same quadratic family sp² − sp − 1 = 0, separated only by the parameter s. At s = 1, you are at the Fibonacci fixed point. As s decreases from 1 toward 0, the positive root passes through every prime in exact order.

The quadratic family sp² − sp − 1 = 0  ← one-parameter family

s = 1:  p = φ = 1.6180339887…  ← Fibonacci · unreachable
s = 1/2:  p = 2  ← first prime
s = 1/6:  p = 3
s = 1/20:  p = 5
s → 0:  p → ∞  ← large primes
